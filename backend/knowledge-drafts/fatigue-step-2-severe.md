## Overview
This branch applies whenever fatigue is accompanied by one-sided weakness or neurological symptoms (possible stroke), a sickle cell crisis pattern, severe shortness of breath or irregular heartbeat, marked pallor, or jaundice. These patterns point toward stroke, severe anemia (including hemolytic crises), or serious infection — all time-sensitive.

## When to Seek Emergency Care
For sudden one-sided weakness, numbness, confusion, or speech/vision/walking difficulty: treat as a stroke and advise the patient to call emergency services or get to the nearest emergency department immediately — do not wait to see if it passes. For known sickle cell disease with severe pain, fever, chest pain, or breathing difficulty: advise going to the hospital or sickle cell clinic right away, as this may be a crisis requiring transfusion or other urgent treatment. For severe shortness of breath, marked pallor, or jaundice: advise urgent evaluation, as these suggest significant anemia that may need transfusion.

## Notes for the Phrasing LLM
For suspected stroke, use direct, urgent language and avoid any hedging — every minute matters. For sickle cell patients, acknowledge that they often recognize their own crisis pattern early and should trust that instinct rather than wait it out at home.
